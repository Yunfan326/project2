Variable selection in regression with compositional covariates

Group 32 PB18000072 李云帆 PB18000106 周子乔

Background

  • Compositional data
    • Compositional data, which consist of the proportions or percentages of a composition, appear frequently in a wide range of applications; examples include geochemical compositions of rocks in geology, household patterns of expenditure in economics, species compositions of biological communities in ecology, and topic compositions of documents in machine learning. The fact that the components of a composition must sum to unity renders many standard multivariate statistical methods inappropriate or inapplicable.
  • Questions and the Log-contrast models
    • Log-contrast models were originally introduced by Aitchison & Bacon-Shone (1984) for modelling experiments with mixtures, and have proved to be useful for a wide variety of regression problems with a composition playing the role of covariate.
    • Suppose that we observe an n-vector y of responses and an \(n × p\) matrix \(X = (x_{ij})\) of covariates, with each row of \(X\) lying in the \((p − 1)\)-dimensional positive simplex \(\,\,S^{p-1}=\{{(x_{1},\cdots,x_{p}):x_{j}>0 \,\,(j=1,2,\cdots,p),\sum_{j=1}^p x_{j}=1}\}\,\,\). Because of the unit-sum constraint, the \(p\) components of a composition cannot vary freely; therefore traditional methodology often requires the omission of certain components to ensure identifiability, and so encounters intrinsic difficulties in providing sensible interpretations for the regression parameters. To resolve these difficulties, Aitchison & Bacon-Shone (1984) proposed applying the log-ratio transformation (Aitchison, 1982) to compositional covariates, resulting in the linear log-contrast model. \[ y=Z^{p}\beta_{\backslash p}^{*}+\varepsilon\,\,\,\,\text{(model 1)}\,\,\,\,\,(1) \]
    • where\(\,\,Z^{p}=\{\text{log}(x_{ij}/x_{ip})\}\,\,\)is the \(\,n × (p − 1)\,\) log-ratio matrix whose \(p\)th component is the reference component, \(\,\,\beta_{\backslash p}^{*}=(\beta_{1}^{*},\cdots,\beta_{p-1}^{*})^{T}\,\,\) is the corresponding \((p − 1)\)-vector of regression coefficients, and \(\,\varepsilon\,\)is an n-vector of independent noise distributed as \(N(0,\sigma^{2})\). By introducing a new coefficient \(\,\,\beta_{p}^{*}=-\sum_{j=1}^{p-1}\beta_{j}^{*}\,\,\), model (1) can be more conveniently expressed in the symmetric form \[ y=Z\beta^{*}+\varepsilon,\,\,\sum_{j=1}^{p}\beta_{j}^{*}=0\,\,\,\,\text{(model 2)}\,\,\,\,\,(2) \]
  • Applying the \(L_{1}\) regularization
    • We do not include an intercept in the model, since it can be eliminated by centring the response and predictor variables. We are concerned with the high dimensional sparse setting, where the dimensionality \(\text{p}\) is comparable to or much larger than the sample size \(\text{n}\), while only a few of the regression coefficients are nonzero.
    • Applying the \(L_{1}\) regularization approach to model (2), we consider the constrained convex optimization problem \[ \hat{\beta}=\text{argmin}_{\beta}(\frac{1}{2n}||y-Z\beta||^{2}+\lambda||\beta||_{1}) \] \[ \text{subject to} \,\,\sum_{j=1}^{p}\beta_j=0\,\,\,\,\,\,(3) \]
    • where \(\beta=(\beta_{1},\beta_{2},\cdots,\beta_{p})^{T}\), \(λ > 0\) is a regularization parameter, and \(||\cdot||_{2}\) and \(||\cdot||_{1}\) denote the \(L_{2}\) norms and \(L_{1}\)norms, respectively.

Optimization algorithm

  • To derive the algorithm, we first form the augmented Lagrangian for the problem \((3)\), \[ L_{\mu}(\beta,\gamma)=\frac{1}{2n}||y-Z\beta||_{2}^{2}+\lambda||\beta||_{1}+\gamma\sum_{j=1}^p\beta_{j}+\frac{\mu}{2}(\sum_{j=1}^{p}\beta_{j})^2 \]

  • where \(\gamma\) is the Lagrange multiplier and \(\mu > 0\) is a penalty parameter. The method of multipliers for the problem consists of the iterations \[ \beta^{k+1}\longleftarrow\text{argmin}_{\beta}L_{\mu}(\beta,\gamma^{k}) \] \[ \gamma^{k+1}\longleftarrow\gamma^{k}+\mu\sum_{j=1}^{p}\beta_{j}^{k+1} \]

  • Define the scaled Lagrange multiplier \(\alpha=\gamma/\mu\) . Then the above iterations can be more conveniently expressed as \[ \beta^{k+1}\longleftarrow\text{argmin}_{\beta}\{\frac{1}{2n}||y-Z\beta||_{2}^{2}+\lambda||\beta||_1+\frac{\mu}{2}(\sum_{j=1}^{p}\beta_{j}+\alpha^{k})^2\}\,\,\,\,\,\,(4) \] \[ \alpha^{k+1}\longleftarrow\alpha^{k}+\sum_{j=1}^{p}\beta_{j}^{k+1}\,\,\,\,\,\,(5) \]

  • Now the \(L_1\) terms in \((4)\) are separable and the subproblem can be solved by coordinate descent. With the other components held fixed, the \(\text{j}\)th component of \(\beta\) is updated by \[ \beta_{j}^{k+1}\longleftarrow\frac{1}{v_{j}+\mu}S_{\lambda} \left( \frac{1}{n}z_{j}^{T}(y-\sum_{i\neq j}\beta_{i}^{k+1}z_{i})-\mu(\sum_{i\neq j}\beta_{i}^{k+1}+\alpha^{k}) \right)\,\,\,\,\,\,(6) \]

  • where \(v_j=||z_j||_2^2/n\) and \(S_{\lambda}=sgn(t)(|t|-\lambda)_{+}\) is the soft thresholding operator. Combining \((4)–(6)\) yields the following coordinate descent method of multipliers for solving problem \((3)\).

  • Algorithm: Coordinate descent method of multipliers

    • Step 1. Initialize \(\beta_{0}\) with 0 or a warm start, \(\alpha^0=0\), \(\mu>0\) , and \(k = 0\) .
    • Step 2. For \(j=1,\cdots,p\), update \(\beta_{j}^{k+1}\) by (6) until convergence.
    • step 3. Update \(\alpha^{k+1}\) by (5).
    • Step 4. Update \(k ← k + 1\) , and repeat Steps 2 and 3 until convergence. Output \(\hat{\beta}=\beta^{k+1}\)

soft<-function(lam,t)#软阈值算子函数
{
  if(t<=-lam){
    return(t+lam)
  }else if(t>=lam){
    return(t-lam)
  }else{
    return (0)
  }
}

        }
        t=(t-mu*(alp+sum))*(1/(1+mu))+beta[i]

get_lamda<-function(y,x,nlam){
  lam.max<-max(abs(crossprod(x,y)))
  lam<-lam.max*exp(seq(0,log(1/nlam),length=nlam))
  return(lam)
}

 get_group_beta<-function(y,x,nlam=100,mu=1,std=TRUE)
{
  if (std) {                        #对数据做标准化
    y <- scale(y, scale=FALSE)
    x <- scale(x, scale=apply(x, 2, sd)*sqrt(nrow(x)-1))
    fac <- 1/attr(x, "scaled:scale")
  } else
    fac <- rep(1, ncol(x))
  lam<-get_lamda(y,x,nlam)        #调用get_lamda函数
  for(i in 1:nlam){
    res$sol[i]<-get_beta(y,x,fac,lam,mu=1,maxit=c(100,100),epis=c(1e-5,1e-5))
    res$lam[i]<-lam[i]
  }
  res
}
  • Tuning parameter selection
    • The regularization parameter \(\lambda\) can be selected by the generalized information criterion for high-dimensional penalized likelihood proposed by Fan & Tang (2013). They showed that the criterion with a uniform choice of the model complexity penalty identifies the true model with probability tending to 1 when the dimensionality p grows at most exponentially with the sample size n. For model (2) and our regularization method, we define \[ \text{GIC}(\lambda)=\text{log}\hat{\sigma}_{\lambda}^2+(s_\lambda-1)\frac{\text{loglog}n}{n}\text{log}(p\vee n) \] where \(\hat{\sigma}_{\lambda}^2=||y-Z\hat{\beta}_\lambda||_2^2/n\), \(\hat{\beta}_\lambda\)is the regularized estimator, \(\text{p}\vee\text{n}=\text{max}(\text{p},\text{n})\), and \(\text{s}_\lambda\) is the number of nonzero coefficients in \(\hat{\beta}_\lambda\). Because of the zero-sum constraint, the effective number of free parameters is \(\text{s}_\lambda-1\) for \(\text{s}_\lambda\geq2\) .

select_GIC <- function(y, x, nlam=100, constr=TRUE) {
  if (constr)
    res <- get_group_beta(y,x,nlam=100,mu=1,std=TRUE)   #有约束对应mu=1
  else
    res <- get_group_beta(y,x,nlam=100,mu=0,std=TRUE)   #有约束对应mu=0
  n <- length(y)
  fit <- log(colMeans((y - matrix(res$int, n, length(res$lam), byrow=TRUE) - x %*% res$sol)^2))
  a <- switch("bic", bic=log(n), aic=2, ft=log(log(n))*log(max(ncol(x), n)))/n
  if (constr)
    gics <- fit + a*(colSums(res$sol != 0) - 1)
  else
    gics <- fit + a*colSums(res$sol != 0)
  ilam <- which.min(gics)                      #找到最优的lamda对应的下标
  list(bet=res$sol[, ilam], lam=res$lam[ilam])
}

Three algorithms

  • Lasso (i) （有零和约束）
    \[ \hat{\beta}_{\backslash p}=\text{argmin}_{\beta_{\backslash p}}(\frac{1}{2n}||y-Z^p \beta_{\backslash p}||_2^2+\lambda||\beta_{\backslash p}||_1) \] \[ \hat{\beta}_p=-1^T\beta_{\backslash p} \]

  • Lasso (ii) （无零和约束） \[ \hat{\beta}=\text{argmin}_{\beta}(\frac{1}{2n}||y-Z\beta||^{2}+\lambda||\beta||_{1}) \]

  • Proposed （有零和约束） \[ \hat{\beta}=\text{argmin}_{\beta}(\frac{1}{2n}||y-Z\beta||^{2}+\lambda||\beta||_{1}) \] \[ \text{subject to} \,\,\sum_{j=1}^{p}\beta_j=0 \]

methods <- function(n=50, p=30, fam="ln", rou=0.2) {
    load(paste("data/dat","n=", n, "p=", p, "rou=", rou, fam, ".rda", sep=" "))
    result <- vector("list",100)
    for (i in 1:100) {
  # Lasso (i)
        k <- sample(1:p, 1)
        new_z <- data[[i]]$z[, -k] - data[[i]]$z[, k]
        ans <- select_GIC(data[[i]]$y, new_z, constr=FALSE)
        result[[i]]$bet1 <- -sum(ans$bet)
        if (k > 1) result[[i]]$bet1 <- c(ans$bet[1:(k - 1)], result[[i]]$bet1)
        if (k < p) result[[i]]$bet1 <- c(result[[i]]$bet1, ans$bet[k:(p - 1)])
        
  # Lasso (ii)
        ans <- select_GIC(data[[i]]$y, data[[i]]$z, constr=FALSE)
        result[[i]]$bet2 <- ans$bet; result[[i]]$lam2 <- ans$lam
        
    # Proposed
        ans <- select_GIC(data[[i]]$y, data[[i]]$z)
        result[[i]]$bet <- ans$bet; result[[i]]$lam <- ans$lam
    }
    save(result, file=paste("data/res","n=", n, "p=", p, "rou=", rou, fam, ".rda", sep=" "))
    print(paste("data/res","n=", n, "p=", p, "rou=", rou, fam, ".rda", sep=" "))
}

Simulations

  • generated the covariate data

    • We generated the covariate data in the following way. We first generated an \(n × p\) data matrix \(W = (w_{i j})\) from a multivariate normal distribution \(N_p(\theta,\Sigma)\), and then obtained the covariate matrix \(X=(x_{i j})\) by the transformation \(x_{i j}=exp(w_{i j})/\sum_{k=1}^pexp(w_{i k})\). The covariates generated thus follow a logistic normal distribution (Aitchison & Shen, 1980). To reflect the fact that the components of a composition in metagenomic data often differ by orders of magnitude, we let \(θ = (θ_ j)\) with \(θ_ j = \text{log}(0·5p)\) for \(j = 1,\cdots,5\) and \(θ_ j=0\) otherwise. To describe different levels of correlations among the components, we let \(\Sigma=(\rho^{|i-j|})\) with \(ρ = 0.2\,\, or \,\,0.5\). We generated the responses according to model (2) with \(β^∗ = (1,-0.8,0.6,0,0,-1.5,-0.5,1.2,0,\cdots,0)^T\) and \(\sigma= 0.5\), so that three of the six nonzero coefficients were among the five major components and the rest were among the minor components.

Conclusion

  • 我们模拟出的结果与原文章非常接近，模拟效果较好。
  • 从上面的表格中可以看出，Lasso (i)在六种性能指标下的性能都较差，这是因为分量没有正则化且总是包含在所选模型中。
  • Lasso (ii)估计优于Lasso (i)，但总是违反零和约束。
  • 文章提出的Proposed估计在PE(prediction error)和Lq loss (q=1,2 and ∞)方面略优于Lasso (ii)。
  • 在(n,p)较小时，Proposed和Lasso (ii)的FP(number of false positives)与FN(number of false negatives)差不多。但在(n,p)很大时，Proposed的FN较小，FP较大。
  • 综合上面的结论，文章提出的Proposed估计与Lasso (i)(ii)相比更有优势。
    • 与Lasso (i)相比，Proposed估计的性能更好。
    • 与Lasso (ii)相比，Proposed满足零和约束，在解决文章开始提出的这类问题时更加适用。
